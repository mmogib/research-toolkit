### Titles to Avoid (with fixes)

**Bad:** "A Study of Some Methods"
**Better:** "Comparison of Gradient Methods for Convex Optimization"

**Bad:** "On the Convergence Properties"
**Better:** "Linear Convergence of the Proximal Point Algorithm"

**Bad:** "New Results in Optimization" (too vague)
**Better:** "Sharp Complexity Bounds for First-Order Methods"

**Bad:** "Algorithms for Problems with Applications to Various Fields" (too broad)
**Better:** "Proximal Algorithms for Sparse Regression in High Dimensions"

## Abstract Examples

### Example 1: Research Paper (Good)

**Title:** "Adaptive Metrics for Norm-Minimization-Based Outer Approximation in Convex Vector Optimization"

**Abstract (180 words):**
> We develop an adaptive-metric framework for norm-minimization-based outer approximation algorithms in bounded convex vector optimization. The key idea is to let the scalarization metric vary across iterations while measuring approximation error in a fixed Euclidean norm, enabling the algorithm to exploit problem geometry dynamically.
>
> Our approach rests on two theoretical foundations. First, we prove that the improved Euclidean convergence rate O(k^{2/(1-q)})—previously known only for the standard ℓ₂ norm—extends to all inner-product norms. Second, we establish a dispersion theorem showing that the cut normals generated by the algorithm naturally spread across all directions when the upper image has a strictly convex boundary with bounded curvature. This geometric condition guarantees that the adaptive metric remains well-conditioned throughout execution.
>
> Building on these results, we derive explicit convergence bounds that quantify how metric conditioning influences the Hausdorff error estimates.
>
> Numerical experiments validate the theoretical rates and demonstrate that adaptive metrics achieve 13–31% fewer iterations than the fixed Euclidean norm while maintaining the same asymptotic rate. Our results provide a rigorous foundation for adaptive metric selection in convex vector optimization.

**Why it works:**
- Opens with main contribution
- Two clear theoretical results stated
- Quantitative experimental result (13-31%)
- No proof techniques mentioned
- Concludes with significance

### Example 2: Before/After Revision

**Before (280 words, problematic):**
> In this paper, we study the convergence properties of a class of optimization algorithms. Optimization is important in many applications including machine learning, signal processing, and control. Many algorithms have been proposed in the literature for solving these problems. We are interested in understanding when these algorithms converge fast.
>
> We consider the problem of minimizing a convex function. By using techniques from convex analysis and exploiting the structure of the Hessian matrix via spectral decomposition, we are able to prove that our algorithm converges. Specifically, we use a clever trick involving the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality together with a contrapositive argument to establish the rate.
>
> Our main theorem shows that the algorithm converges at a certain rate under some assumptions. We also prove several lemmas that are needed for the main result. The proofs are quite technical and involve careful analysis of the iterates.
>
> We present some numerical experiments that show our algorithm works well in practice. The experiments were run on a laptop computer and took several hours to complete.
>
> In conclusion, we have made some contributions to the study of optimization algorithms.

**Problems:**
- Opens with "In this paper, we study..." (weak)
- Excessive background (paragraph 1)
- Describes proof techniques (paragraph 2)
- Vague about results ("a certain rate", "some assumptions")
- Irrelevant details (laptop, several hours)
- Weak conclusion

**After (150 words, improved):**
> We prove that proximal gradient methods achieve linear convergence rate O(ρᵏ) for minimizing strongly convex functions with Lipschitz gradients, where ρ = 1 - μ/L depends only on the condition number.
>
> Our analysis establishes three results. First, we derive the tight convergence factor ρ = (L-μ)/(L+μ), improving the previously known bound. Second, we show that Nesterov acceleration achieves the optimal rate O((1-√(μ/L))ᵏ) without requiring knowledge of μ. Third, we extend these results to composite objectives with non-smooth regularizers.
>
> Numerical experiments on sparse regression problems with n = 10⁶ variables confirm the predicted rates and demonstrate 3× speedup over standard gradient descent.
>
> These results provide the sharpest known convergence guarantees for proximal methods and establish a unified framework for analyzing accelerated variants.

**Improvements:**
- Opens with main result
- Specific rates given
- Three clear contributions
- Quantitative experiments
- Strong conclusion

### Example 3: Thesis Abstract (PhD)

**Title:** "Theory and Algorithms for Convex Vector Optimization: Approximation, Duality, and Computation"

**Abstract (420 words):**
> This dissertation develops theoretical foundations and computational methods for convex vector optimization problems (CVOPs), where multiple competing objectives must be simultaneously optimized over a convex feasible region.
>
> The central challenge in CVOPs is that, unlike scalar optimization, there is no single optimal solution but rather a set of Pareto-efficient solutions representing different trade-offs among objectives. We address this challenge through three main contributions spanning approximation theory, duality, and algorithms.
>
> First, we establish approximation-theoretic foundations for outer approximation methods. We prove that polyhedral outer approximations of the upper image converge at rate O(k^{2/(1-q)}) under inner-product norms, where k is the iteration count and q is the number of objectives. This rate is optimal among methods using linear scalarizations. We further show that adaptive selection of the scalarization norm can reduce iteration counts by 15-30% in practice while preserving the theoretical rate.
>
> Second, we develop a comprehensive duality theory for CVOPs with geometric constraints. We introduce a Lagrangian dual problem whose optimal value characterizes the minimal distance from a reference point to the upper image. This duality framework yields both theoretical insights—including strong duality under constraint qualification—and practical algorithms based on dual decomposition.
>
> Third, we design and analyze efficient algorithms for large-scale CVOPs. Our primal algorithm extends the norm-minimization approach of Ararat, Ulus, and Umer to handle problems with thousands of variables and tens of objectives. Our dual algorithm exploits problem structure through coordinate descent on the dual, achieving linear convergence under strong duality. Implementations in Julia demonstrate scalability to problems 10× larger than previously tractable.
>
> We validate our theoretical results through extensive computational experiments on portfolio optimization, radiation therapy planning, and engineering design problems. The experiments confirm the predicted convergence rates and demonstrate that our algorithms outperform existing methods on problems with more than three objectives.
>
> The contributions of this dissertation advance both the theory and practice of convex vector optimization. Our approximation results settle open questions about achievable convergence rates. Our duality theory provides new tools for analyzing CVOPs. Our algorithms enable practitioners to solve problems of unprecedented scale. Together, these contributions establish a foundation for the next generation of multiobjective optimization methods.

**Why it works:**
- Clear thesis statement (central challenge)
- Three distinct contributions clearly enumerated
- Specific results with rates and percentages
- Validation described
- Strong concluding paragraph on significance
- Appropriate length for PhD (420 words)
